# Module 01: Phishing & Social Engineering Defense

Phishing and social engineering remain the most common initial access vector in real-world breaches.
This module is prioritized first because it addresses the highest-frequency, highest-impact risk category for any organization, regardless of size or industry.

## Compliance Mapping

| Framework |	Control |	Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| NIST CSF | PR.AT-1 |	All users are informed and trained on recognizing social engineering attempts
|ISO 27001:2022	| A.6.3	| Personnel shall receive appropriate awareness education and training relevant to their job function

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1. Identify the core psychological triggers used in social engineering attacks
2. Recognize the technical indicators of a phishing email
3. Apply a consistent decision process when encountering a suspicious message
4. Report a suspected phishing attempt through the correct internal channel

### Content
What is Social Engineering

Social engineering is the manipulation of a person, rather than a system, to gain unauthorized access to information or resources. 
Attackers exploit natural human tendencies such as trust, urgency, fear, and the desire to be helpful.
Phishing is the most common delivery method, but the same principles apply to phone-based attacks (vishing), text message attacks (smishing), and in-person impersonation.

Every social engineering attempt relies on at least one of these triggers:
- **Urgency**: "Your account will be locked in 24 hours"
- **Authority**: An email appearing to come from a senior executive or IT department
- **Fear**: "Suspicious activity detected on your account"
- **Curiosity or reward**: "You have won a prize" or "Invoice attached"
  
### **Recognizing a Phishing Email**

A phishing email will typically show a combination of the following indicators.
No single indicator is proof on its own, but two or more together are a strong warning sign:

- Sender address does not match the organization it claims to represent
- Generic greeting instead of your name
- Request to click a link or open an attachment urgently
- Mismatched or suspicious URL when hovering over a link
- Poor grammar or unusual phrasing, though this is less reliable with AI-generated phishing content
- Request for credentials, payment details, or sensitive data

### **The Decision Process**

When you receive a message that raises any doubt, follow this process before taking any action:

1. **Pause**: Do not click, reply, or download anything immediately.
2. **Verify the sender**: Check the actual email address, not just the display name.
3. **Check the request independently**: If the message claims to be from a colleague or executive asking for something unusual, contact them directly through a separate, known channel, not by replying to the message.
4. **Report**: Use the organization's designated reporting channel, such as a "Report Phishing" button or a security team mailbox, even if you are not certain it is malicious.

**Reporting a false positive carries no penalty. Failing to report a real attempt does.**

## Summary

Social engineering succeeds by exploiting human psychology, not technical vulnerabilities.
Recognizing the common triggers and applying a consistent pause-verify-report process significantly reduces organizational risk, regardless of the specific technical sophistication of an attack.
